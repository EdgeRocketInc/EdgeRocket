The Perils and Promise of Individual Development Plans

All too often, tools used to help employees’ performance – as well as their personal development – are viewed warily. Exhibit number one: the Individual Development Plan. These tools for personal and professional advancement can inspire annoyance and skepticism. But, used correctly, they can be an excellent resource and critical difference maker in your company’s culture and growth.

Your company may not use the term “Individual Development Plan” (or “IDP” for short). Some organizations call it an Employee Development Plan or just a development plan, or may generically refer to “learning and development goals.” The process and details vary, but the principles remain the same: helping each person in your company identify learning opportunities that will enable them to grow and excel.

Implementation can be as different as the terminology. Some companies integrate development planning with their performance review process, rigorously enforcing the use of development plans and goals. Other organizations don’t utilize IDPs at all. And most fall somewhere in between -- promoting the use of development plans among managers and employees, but not following through and realizing their full potential.

An Individual Development Plan is fairly straightforward in name. An IDP is designed to set short and long term goals for employees, supervisors, and upper management alike. Rather than specifying business goals and results, an IDP is usually focused more on a person’s plans for improving their knowledge, skills and competencies. As the person improves their skills, their work performance improves as well. When companies enlist IDPs for all employees, the results are higher growth for everyone involved.

The components of an IDP are often based on the following:

  • What kind of projects and experiences will help you improve your performance?
  • What kind of relationships do you want to build?
  • What knowledge and skills do you plan to gain?
  • Are there classes, seminars, or continuing education you want to participate in?

Even the most seasoned employees in your company have several topic areas or skills they could improve. A sample IDP may contain development goals such as the following:

  • Gaining more advanced industry knowledge
  • Improving skills in creating slides and giving presentations
  • Attaining a better understanding of new-ish fields like data science
  • Mastering basic knowledge of accounting and finance

Ideally, an employee’s IDP contains the basic elements of smart goals: specific and measurable plans, aligned with company strategy, that can be achieved within a designated timeline (like a 3- or 6-month timeframe). The action steps often include identifying projects to work on, coordinating meetings with people or experts, or completing classes, books, or other learning content. For best results, an IDP should be reviewed and refreshed on a quarterly basis between employee and direct supervisor.

IDPs don’t have to be stressful or onerous. If utilized correctly -- with realistic goals and frequent updates -- they can be a powerful planning tool that boosts motivation and accountability, and benefits the employee and company alike.
