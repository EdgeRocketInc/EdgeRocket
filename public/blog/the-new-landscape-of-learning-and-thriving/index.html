The New Landscape of Learning (and Thriving!)

Several years ago, I worked for a technology company - I was part of the consulting team.  Unfortunately, the company’s formerly rapid growth had recently begun to decline (sharply), and the executive team made a big and not-very-fun decision: shed the consulting team that I was a part of, and focus on the core technology business.  Many of my consulting colleagues were let go.  A few of us were invited to stay on in a different position.  For me, that meant moving from consulting into a product management role.

I had never done product management at a technology company.  But I thought it’d be a good opportunity, so I went for it.  I knew the industry and our product.  My (new) boss was smart and helpful.  And I was an avid learner, so I figured I could get up to speed in short order.  

So I began to do research on product management.  I spent many hours entering queries into Google’s search box to identify articles, books, and other materials to help me master my new trade.  

What I discovered was that great resources were few and far between.  Sure, I did find a handful of useful materials.  I bought a few books from Amazon (including Inspired and The Product Manager’s Desk Reference), and they were very helpful.  I read a few dozen articles, which helped to fill in some knowledge gaps.  I got information from the websites of product management industry associations (including AIPMM, SVPMA, PDMA).  And, of course, I had numerous meetings and conversations with my boss and colleagues about our activities.  

But overall, my knowledge quest was frustrating.  I wanted more.  I needed more details on product management approaches, and more specific examples from experienced product managers at great companies.  What are the most effective ways to conduct customer interviews?  How should I interact with the internal teams I was working with?  How can I influence and manage people that don’t report to me?  Even though I was hungry and ready to invest whatever time and energy necessary to learn, there just was not enough high-quality, expert material available.  

I dived in and did everything I could to acquire the most needed knowledge and skills, and was able to ramp up reasonably well, and make good progress on the company product initiatives.  

But here’s the point: I could have contributed in a much more significant way if I had had access to better learning resources and latest-and-greatest information on product management.  I was playing a pivotal role, helping to guide a major turnaround of our business.  Our team was making critical decisions about our products and strategy, and we were re-directing the company's staffing plans and work activities.  More in-depth product management content would have deepened our understanding, given us more ideas for specific tactics, and helped us achieve better outcomes.      

Fast forward to today.  If a similar situation occurred now, and if I were that same consultant transitioning into a product manager role, I could access a wealth of learning resources - most of them available online.  Not all of these resources are easy-to-find or good - in fact there is a lot of crappy, time-wasting content.  But with some exploration, you can find excellent learning resources by product management pros.  And these learning resources can directly contribute to enhanced performance and results. 

“Learning resources” is vague, so let me be specific.  Here is a list of short courses, articles, videos and other information that would have been extremely useful to me as I was undergoing my job transition years ago:

Be A Great Product Leader  | Adam Nash, former PM at LinkedIn | Slide deck | Slideshare 

Lean Product Management | Dan Olsen, former PM at Google & Intuit | Video | Google Ventures 

Good Product Manager/Bad Product Manager | Ben Horowitz | Article/Document 

Ideas About Innovation | Marissa Mayer, former PM at Google and CEO of Yahoo | Video | YouTube

A Product Manager's Job | Josh Elman, PM at Twitter and FaceBook | Article | Medium

How to Get a Job in Product Management | Jason Shah, PM at Yammer | Online Course | Udemy

Skillsets to Shift Your Career to Product Management | Raj Karamchedu | Online Course | Udemy

Product Management for Non-Product Managers | David Lifson, former PM at Etsy | Video | General Assembly

Prioritization for Product Managers | David Lifson, former PM at Etsy | Video | General Assembly

What Distinguishes the Top 1% of Product Managers | Q&A Forum | Quora

In addition to these items, I would have taken some technology courses to enrich my technical knowledge, and to help me communicate more effectively with our engineering team.  I likely would have chosen a variety of courses from Coursera, edX, Treehouse, or Code School.

The “curriculum” listed above would have represented a meaningful time investment.  It’s a lot of material.  But having access to these resources - taking the courses, reading the articles, watching the videos - would have made a major difference in my effectiveness.

My plight a number of years ago illustrates a profound transformation that has occurred: today there is an amazing array of online courses and resources that enable people to acquire valuable knowledge and job skills.  Whether you just took on a new role in your career, or you’re sharpening your skills to better execute your current responsibilities - you have an unprecedented arsenal of resources available to help you learn, grow, and succeed.

May 12, 2014, by Peter McLaughlin
