The Cost of Employee Turnover - and How to Minimize It

There are a number of different algorithms and formulas to calculate the cost of having to replace an employee. All have minor variations and you can find many HR professionals debating details such as how you should calculate ‘pre-departure costs’ or ‘orientation and training costs.’ While each formula has its merits the conclusion is always the same: The cost of employee turnover is much higher than maintaining a long-lasting employee base.

So how much does employee turnover cost?

In a 2012 study from the Center for American Progress, the cost to replace a single employee was roughly 20% of the position’s annual salary. Other studies found turnover costs to be 30-50% of the position’s salary. To break it down into actual dollars, the average cost to replace an $8 per hour employee can range from $3,500 to a staggering $11,000. This is only for a slightly-higher-than-minimum wage job. The higher the salary and skill level it takes to perform the responsibilities of the job, the higher the cost becomes to replace that salary and position.

These costs come in many ways, such as the production lost during the new employee’s training, recruitment costs, lost productivity while the position is open, overtime paid to other employees to maintain production standards, interviewing costs, etc. Many businesses don’t take these aspects into consideration when dealing with turnover.

Maintaining an engaged workforce who want to stick around for the long haul is the key to keeping your company’s turnover costs to a minimum. Contrary to what some may think, keeping your employees happy doesn’t necessarily mean you have to give big raises every year or create a number of expensive incentives in the hopes your employees will stick with you and your team.

Compensation and benefits are certainly a significant ingredients in retention, but many other job facets are equally or more important. Two other critical factors: (1) employees want to feel that their boss and team are competent and collaborative, and (2) employees want to learn and grow. Because the world around us is changing so rapidly, ongoing training has become vital to sustaining a high-performance workforce. The more your staff is learning and growing, the more likely they are to keep their skillsets with the company who helped guide them and train them.

New information is now almost a constant. No matter the position in your organization there are always new and better ways to perform. There are always new programs and more efficient ways of enabling your employees to not just show up to work everyday, but to do so with enthusiasm, purpose, and commitment.
